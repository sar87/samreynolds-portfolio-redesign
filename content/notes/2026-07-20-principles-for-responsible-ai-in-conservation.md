Last week I finally released the pre-print of a paper that has been the product of a highly collaborative effort with many other conservation AI researchers around the world ([https://doi.org/10.33774/coe-2026-5lcdd](https://doi.org/10.33774/coe-2026-5lcdd)). The paper is called "**Principles for navigating responsible use of AI for conservation".** Annoyingly, I forgot to expand AI in the title to Artificial Intelligence - but this is something that is easily fixed. The paper has also been submitted to the journal [Conservation Science and Practice](https://conbio.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/25784854?gad_campaignid=22794000390&gad_source=1&gbraid=0AAAAADoE1amViGwrSjaL83jlXaqjkh4_B&gclid=Cj0KCQjwjvfSBhDpARIsAEiOpSsCJeRiTthuLZUtp2_Zeeb335TEOjdOCvC40XEDFxUMUTdKmz2wegsaAsXDEALw_wcB&utm_campaign=R3MR425&utm_content=LifeSciences&utm_medium=cpc&utm_source=google), published by the Society for Conservation Biology. 

### The genesis of the paper

This paper arose from a week-long workshop I was invited to attend titled "Shaping the Future of AI for Conservation" that was hosted at the [Oxford Martin School](https://www.oxfordmartin.ox.ac.uk/) at the [University of Oxford](https://www.ox.ac.uk/).  The workshop was funded by [Schmidt Sciences](https://www.schmidtsciences.org/) coordinated and organised by [Dr Amy Hinsley](https://www.biology.ox.ac.uk/people/amy-hinsley), [Dr Rachel Parkinson](https://www.biology.ox.ac.uk/people/rachel-parkinson), [Lucy Radford](https://www.biology.ox.ac.uk/people/lucy-radford) and [Dr Lauren Coad](https://iccs.org.uk/author/lauren-coad/). 

There were over 180 applications to attend the workshop from conservation researchers and practitioners from around the world. We were sorted into one of three themes which would be the focus of our discussions throughout the week:

1. Predicting conservation threats using social data
2. Building foundation models for ecology
3. Ensuring equity and inclusion in AI use and application

I was assigned to the latter. We had a fantastic group of participants in our group, with a geographic spread from Indonesia to Kenya to Brazil, all working in conservation research or practice and thinking about the integration of AI into this space.

As the days went on, we clarified our thinking and discussions. As anyone who has been part of workshop discussions before, we all know it takes a little while for everyone to start talking the same language. Here is where it is important to have a good framing for people to engage with. We soon identified that the conversations we needed to have were not at the level of technology use in conservation, which it is easy to end up talking about, but specifically about what new challenges are raised by introducing an AI element. For example, the use of camera traps and the issues concerning human bycatch, privacy and appropriate data handling are distinct from the specific challenges that enter the picture when you are using classifiers to automatically categorise images, sending data to third party models or using the data to train new models. 

Throughout the week we landed on a useful framework for discussion where we discussed each of the stages of a conservation project, from: Defining the question/problem; deciding experimental/project design; Model deployment; Data collection; Output and analysis; Using data for decision making. This helped us to get our thoughts out in the open for discussion. The structure of the paper then shifted towards a three part structure where we discussed the distinct ethical challenges around the Techical, the Social, and the Legal aspects of AI deployment. This is where we were at the end of the workshop.

### After the workshop

This was the focus of the initial draft. As anyone who has worked on multi-author papers will tell you, it is a constant wrangling of deciding what should be in and what should be out. Making sure that all voices are heard equally and contributions are not dismissed. Luckily, having the week long workshop allowed us to do a lot of this discussion in person, rather than over email and through google doc comments. 

Once we had an initial draft which followed this three-part format Technical, Social and Legal, we looked to bring in Chris Sandbrook - who had intended to be at the workshop but could no longer make the dates when the event came around. Chris gave us a lot of great feedback on the initial draft which led to a fundamental reframing of the entire paper. At this point we decided to hang our thinking from the framework that Chris had developed with colleagues on establishing principles for the responsible use of surveillance technology in conservation. 

This really helped to align a lot of the thinking that we had within the original draft and enormously improved the layout of the arguments and sets out the issues much more clearly for the reader. 

This version of the paper went through three or four rounds of iterations and the final draft (version 7), was the version we released as a preprint. 

### Next steps

The paper is currently online as a preprint, hosted on Cambridge Universities 'Open Engage' platform. We are hoping that the paper will be considered for publication at Conservation Science and Practice, as this is where Chris' original paper on the principles of the responsible use of technology in conservation was published. 

We are also keen to hear what people think of the preprint so that we might be able to improve the final version of the paper if it is considered for publication.
