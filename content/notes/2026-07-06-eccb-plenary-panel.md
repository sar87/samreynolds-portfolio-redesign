ECCB has kicked off. I missed the plenary talks, but arrived in time for their panel discussion. My main takeaway is that I am pretty sure I have heard all of these things before, and it is hard to know how these insights are supposed to translate into action. 

1. Biodiversity is still declining, however there have been successes: what are the ingredients for successful biodiversity conservation policies/actions?

- Dont preach - people don't want to be preached to
- We need strong policy frameworks, a good policy framework needs to be implementable. You need the resources in society, government and business in order to be able to implement. There is a gap between what the law says and the ability of member states to meet those needs.
- Reframing around that we arent saving nature for natures sake, 'nature' will be fine - but we need to save it in order to save ourselves. 

2. 2030 is coming, and many targets will not be reached - what should we prioritise post 2030?

- We need to focus on drivers, and bending our financial solutions towards what is good for biodiversity. 
- We need to focus on implementation - institutions spend too much time adapting and modifying policies without doing any of the work of implementation. There is a mismatch between national and local governments in terms of the ability to push through conservation implementation action. Denmark is a good example of a country doing this, that has moved conservation beyond party politics. 

3. Are we communicating the importance and consequences of biodiversity decline clearly enough?

- We have done a good job of trying to frame biodiversity loss as something that is integral to life on earth - but not what is understood, is why is it is important to people and their lives. Perhaps we should be more closely linking their effects on health? Or crop production? The importance of pollinators on food supply is one example. She frames this as the Environmentalist Paradox - all metrics for human health and wealth are improving, so people do not perceive that there is this existential risk to human health. Biodiversity loss is not physically perceived in the same way as the climate crisis, and this makes it harder to communicates in a visceral way. 

- The people most hurt by climate change and biodiversity loss, are those who are most adversely affected by conservation and climate policies. The policies we have set in motion are accessible to people who earn a lot of money, affecting the price of primary goods in a way that can be absorbed by the rich but not the poor. The underlying socio-economic problem will prevent the uptake of these policies, we can't let these questions be pulled into the 'culture wars', otherwise it will only be autocratic countries who will be able to make any headway on biodiversity policy.

4. How can we improve the coherence across different policy areas to reach the biodiversity targets?

- We need to collaborate across ministries. e.g., In the Netherlands they looked at target 11, on harmful subsidies for biodiversity, as many of these subsidies are not always held by environmental ministries.
- Improving coherence requires more money. In France, there is a beautiful reserve, which is neighboured by an industrial zone -  rather than supplying this area with green energy through overland powerlines, we need to have buried cables, which cost a lot more money to install. We need more money so that these tradeoffs do not become blockers. 
- Look to other successes, make sure that all stakeholders are given the 'full measure', so that they can collaborate in a way that enables them to prioritise biodiversity. 

## QnA

1. What are examples that you have seen from other people that will allow us to be able to communicate with 'policy makers'. 

- Pushing for planting trees and greening cities is something you can push for as an individual. Lower temperatures and give biodiversity an opportunity to thrive. 
- Start small, you dont have to worry that you arent fluent in the policy making language the first time you do - but practice. Take up a single issue, talk to one person about it. Pick an issue that is close to your heart, and find an issue to champion.
- No green deal without the Friday of Future movement - and this is middle schoolers! 

2. How are you as policy makers, are you waiting for us to present our work? Please give concrete examples. 

- I want a succinct overview of a global issues. Due to the issues in the straight of hormuz, and issues with fertilizer supply, we are looking at alternative forms of fertiliser, such as digestates. Which is in itself a complicated landscape. I need something which covers not just biodiversity, but the economic impact. 

2. How are you going to explain to the global south that they need to preserve biodiversity when they are the first to affected - how are you going to reinforce the science to keep things moving

- I suggest the book "The Roots of the Sky" - 
- Our future depends on a large extent on what happens in Africa - the electification of Africa is a huge opportunity for Africa, and our ability to work with Africa. Nigeria is going to be one of the largest countries on the planet in 20-30 years, as climate changes, this will prove a larger and larger issue. 

3. This is the first time I am hearing about the Convention on Biological Diversity - because I am an Undergrad in the USA. How do you help with misinformation around biodiversity. 

- What you can do as a citizen is the same as in other countries - don't make it too big. Something small, like, more nature in the surroundings allows for cooler local climate and a positive impact on house prices.
- You have to be able to react with facts, so that you can fight misinformation. It is important that you bring the right facts and numbers to the table.
